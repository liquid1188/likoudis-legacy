In Memoriam

James Likoudis

December 11, 1928 — September 3, 2024

Theologian. Apologist. Ecumenist. Over seven decades as a Catholic, James Likoudis became one of the most prolific lay defenders of the papal magisterium and one of the foremost Catholic voices in dialogue with Eastern Orthodoxy. Born into a Greek immigrant family, baptized in the Greek Orthodox Church, and received into the Catholic Church in 1952, his life traced the very arc of reunion he spent it advocating for.

Early Life & Conversion

James Likoudis was born on December 11, 1928, in Lackawanna, New York, to Greek immigrant parents. Raised in the Greek Orthodox Church, he came of age in the ethnic Greek community of western New York. His encounter with Catholicism came through the Newman Club at the University of Buffalo, where he engaged the writings of the Church Fathers and the scholastic tradition. In 1952, at the age of 23, he was received into full communion with Rome — a decision that would define the rest of his life.

His conversion was not merely personal. It became a vocation. As a man who had lived inside Eastern Orthodoxy, who spoke its liturgical language and understood its theological instincts from within, Likoudis was uniquely positioned to serve as a Catholic interlocutor — not a polemicist, but a patient and deeply informed voice for unity.

"He is himself a convert, and he recognizes that conversion — any conversion — is not the matter of a moment. It is the love of a lifetime."

— Scott Hahn, PhD, President, St. Paul Center for Biblical Theology

Educator & Lecturer

Likoudis spent significant years as an educator, teaching history and government at Rosary Hill College (now Daemen University) in the late 1960s and 1970s. His classroom work was inseparable from his apostolate: he lectured widely at parishes, conferences, seminaries, and universities across the United States on the themes of papal primacy, ecumenism, and Catholic moral teaching. He was a regular presence at the conferences of the Latin Liturgy Association and Catholics United for the Faith.

His appearance on the Mother Angelica Live program in November 1996 brought his scholarship to a national audience, making accessible to ordinary Catholics the complex theological terrain of Catholic-Orthodox dialogue.

Catholics United for the Faith

Likoudis served for many years as president of Catholics United for the Faith (CUF), a lay organization founded in 1968 to support and defend the Church's magisterium in the turbulent aftermath of the Second Vatican Council. Under his leadership, CUF became a significant voice for fidelity to the ordinary magisterium at a time when dissent had become increasingly organized and visible.

His work at CUF also shaped his approach to liturgical questions. He was a consistent defender of the Novus Ordo against both those who attacked it as invalid and those who performed it carelessly. His essays from this period — many of which are preserved in the Foundation's archive — remain among the most nuanced treatments of the postconciliar liturgical situation available from a lay perspective.

Catholic-Orthodox Ecumenism

The central thread of Likoudis' scholarly life was the question of Catholic-Orthodox reunion. His trilogy on the Byzantine tradition — The Divine Primacy of the Bishop of Rome and Modern Eastern Orthodoxy, Eastern Orthodoxy and the See of Peter, and Ending the Byzantine Greek Schism — constitutes the most sustained lay-scholarly engagement with these questions in twentieth-century American Catholic letters.

He worked not by assertion but by argument: engaging Orthodox theologians on their own terms, tracing the historical record of the early councils, and showing with patient precision where the Eastern objections to Rome rest on contestable historical claims. His goal was always reunion — not capitulation, but the recognition of a unity that already exists in principle and awaits full visible expression.

"In the field of Catholic-Orthodox dialogue, James Likoudis figures as one of the most prominent Catholic apologists."

— Jose Pereira, PhD, Professor Emeritus of Theology, Fordham University

Family & Personal Life

James Likoudis was married to Ruth Likoudis, his lifelong companion and partner in apostolate. Together they met Pope St. John Paul II and Mother Teresa of Calcutta — encounters that reflect the depth of the Catholic network in which they moved. He was the grandfather of Andrew Likoudis, founder of the Likoudis Legacy Foundation, and the intellectual forebear of a generation of Catholic scholars who drew on his work.

Recognition & Legacy

In 2002, Likoudis received the Blessed Frederick Ozanam Award for Catholic Social Action from the Society for Catholic Social Scientists. In 2020, Sacred Heart Major Seminary awarded him an honorary doctorate of divinity in recognition of his lifetime of service to Catholic scholarship. He died on September 3, 2024, at the age of 95, having witnessed the full arc of the postconciliar Church from its hopeful beginnings through its crises and its renewals.

The Likoudis Legacy Foundation was established to ensure that his corpus — over 300 essays, fourteen books, and a lifetime of correspondence with bishops, theologians, and ordinary Catholics — remains accessible to the next generation of scholars and the faithful.

"Likoudis occupies a singular place in the literature of his time. Faithful, yet not crazy. Very few people got it so right."

— Mike Aquilina, Executive Vice President, St. Paul Center for Biblical Theology
