In Memoriam

James Likoudis

December 11, 1928 — September 3, 2024
Theologian Apologist Historian Ecumenist Educator Convert

Over seven decades as a Catholic, James Likoudis became one of the most productive and clear-headed lay defenders of the papal magisterium in twentieth-century American Catholicism. Born to Greek immigrants in Lackawanna, New York, baptized in the Greek Orthodox Church, and received into full communion with Rome at twenty-three, his life traced the very arc of reunion he spent it advocating for. He wrote fourteen books, over 300 essays, debated in packed town halls, appeared on national television, lectured at seminaries and universities, shaped campaigns that reached the desks of Cardinal Ratzinger and the Pontifical Council for the Family — and remained, by every account, a man of warmth, humor, and personal holiness.

Origins: Lackawanna and the Greek Orthodox Church

James Likoudis was born on December 11, 1928, in Lackawanna, New York — a working-class steel town on the southern edge of Buffalo, built largely by immigrant communities from southern and eastern Europe. His parents were Greek immigrants, and he was raised in the Greek Orthodox Church, immersed from birth in the theological world of Byzantine Christianity: its liturgical cadences, its patristic sensibility, its fierce attachment to the conciliar tradition.

That formation never left him. In a significant sense, the whole of his subsequent Catholic life was shaped by the fact that he knew Eastern Christianity not from the outside but from within — as something he had prayed, inhabited, and eventually left behind for a reason he spent decades trying to articulate with precision and charity.

Conversion: Buffalo, St. Thomas Aquinas, and 1952

The decisive turn came at the University of Buffalo's Newman Club, where Likoudis encountered, apparently for the first time in depth, the Catholic intellectual tradition. The writings of St. Thomas Aquinas and Christopher Dawson — systematic, historically grounded, confident in reason's capacity to serve faith — opened a world that his Greek Orthodox upbringing had not provided in quite that form. The encounter was intellectual, but the conversion it produced was thoroughly personal.

In 1952, at twenty-three, James Likoudis was received into full communion with Rome. He had found, as he later described in his 1988 essay "To Be Truly Orthodox Is to Be in Communion with Peter's See," not an abandonment of Eastern Christianity but its proper completion. His model for this journey was St. Leopold of Castelnuovo — the gentle Capuchin confessor from the Adriatic coast who spent his life quietly reconciling souls to God and to one another. That patron saint choice says something precise about what Likoudis understood his own vocation to be.

"He is himself a convert, and he recognizes that conversion — any conversion — is not the matter of a moment. It is the love of a lifetime."

— Scott Hahn, PhD, President, St. Paul Center for Biblical Theology

Educator: Twenty Years in the Classroom

Likoudis spent more than two decades as a teacher. He taught social studies to high school students and served as a history and government instructor at St. John's of the Atonement, the Franciscan minor seminary in New York, and at Rosary Hill College (now Daemen University) in upstate New York. Former students remembered him years later as an engaging and demanding teacher who insisted on learning to think rather than merely to recite. He lectured seminarians as well — a fact not lost on the priests who later cited his influence.

In 1977, he translated Renée Casin's St. Thomas Aquinas: Orthodoxy, and Neo-Modernism in the Church from French into English — an early indication of his range as a scholar willing to work in service of ideas, regardless of whether they bore his own name.

Catholics United for the Faith

Likoudis's most sustained institutional work was at Catholics United for the Faith (CUF), the lay apostolate founded in 1968 — the same year as Humanae Vitae and the same year organized Catholic dissent began to take institutional form. He served CUF for more than twenty-five years, eventually as president. He also founded Credo of Buffalo.

At CUF, his work was simultaneously doctrinal, pastoral, and strategic. He was a leading voice for fidelity to Humanae Vitae at a time when that required real institutional courage. He traveled and lectured worldwide. He ran packed town halls on abortion and parental rights in the 1970s and 1980s — crowds lined up outside the doors. And he pursued specific campaigns with a tenacity that produced concrete results at the level of the Vatican itself.

His campaign against Christ Among Us — a catechism widely used in Catholic religious education that contained serious theological errors — eventually reached Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger and the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith. The CDF acted to remove the book's Imprimatur and Nihil Obstat and ultimately requested that Paulist Press cease further publication. He played a comparable role in bringing to the Vatican's attention the theological modernism being taught by Charles Curran at Catholic University of America; Curran was eventually removed from his teaching position by the Church hierarchy.

He also represented CUF at a Vatican meeting convened by Cardinal Alfonso López Trujillo, President of the Pontifical Council for the Family. That meeting produced the foundational document Truth and Meaning of Human Sexuality: Guidelines for Education within the Family — a text that would shape Catholic catechetical policy on sexual education for decades.

Morality in Media and the Culture Wars

Likoudis served as president of Morality in Media — the organization now known as the National Center on Sexual Exploitation — and as moderator of the New York television series Sex and Morality. While living in Buffalo, he received the Morality in Media award alongside Mother Angelica herself, in recognition of his sustained public efforts against the degradation of American media culture.

He worked closely with Dietrich von Hildebrand and Alice von Hildebrand through their organization Veil of Innocence, serving as a board member, lecturer, and contributor. He wrote an essay for Dietrich's Sex Education: The Basic Issues and Related Essays — a volume that carries, inside its front pages, a personal letter of endorsement handwritten by Mother Teresa of Calcutta.

The Ecumenical Trilogy

The deepest and most original current of Likoudis's scholarly life was Catholic-Orthodox ecumenism. His trilogy on the Byzantine tradition constitutes the most sustained lay-scholarly engagement with these questions produced in twentieth-century American Catholic letters.

The Divine Primacy of the Bishop of Rome and Modern Eastern Orthodoxy: Letters to a Greek Orthodox on the Unity of the Church engaged Orthodox objections to papal supremacy and infallibility at the level of patristics and conciliar history. An earlier edition responded specifically to Eastern Orthodox writer Michael Whelton's arguments in Two Paths. Eastern Orthodoxy and the See of Peter: A Journey Towards Full Communion (2006) extended the argument across the historical record. Ending the Byzantine Greek Schism addressed the origins and perpetuation of the great rupture with the precision of a historian and the pastoral urgency of a man who had lived on both sides.

He worked not by condescension but by argument — taking Orthodox theologians seriously enough to engage them on their own terms, tracing the patristic record with care, and showing where the historical objections to Rome rest on contestable foundations. The goal was never victory in debate but reunion in truth. By his own estimate, and by the testimony of those who knew his correspondence, he helped bring hundreds — possibly thousands — of people into full communion with the Catholic Church over his lifetime.

"James Likoudis really does occupy a singular place in the literature of his time; faithful, yet not crazy. Very few people got it so right."

— Mike Aquilina, Executive Vice President, St. Paul Center for Biblical Theology

The Defense of Vatican II

Perhaps the most widely read of Likoudis's books is The Pope, the Council, and the Mass: Answers to Questions the Traditionalists Have Asked, co-authored with Kenneth Whitehead. Published in 1981, it was a direct, charitable, and thoroughly documented response to the Traditionalist critique of the Second Vatican Council and the Novus Ordo Mass — addressing precisely the arguments being made by followers of Archbishop Lefebvre and others who contested the Council's authority.

Likoudis brought a specific authority to this work: he was no liberal accommodationist. His entire career had been spent defending the magisterium against progressive dissent. His argument for the Council was not that it represented a rupture with tradition but exactly the opposite — that to reject the Council was to reject the very principle of magisterial authority that traditionalists claimed to be defending. The book has never been adequately answered.

Friendships, Vatican Visits, and the Catholic Network

Likoudis helped Father John Hardon, SJ found the organization Eternal Life, and contributed to Hardon's books — penning the foreword to Hardon's published doctoral dissertation on "Bellarmine's Doctrine of the Relation of Sincere Non-Catholics to the Catholic Church."

He was a mentor to Jeffrey Mirus of CatholicCulture.org, who credits Likoudis with shaping his early formation as a Catholic apologist. He was a close friend of Scott Hahn. He traveled to Rome regularly — meeting Pope St. John Paul II on multiple occasions, presenting him with his books, and on at least one occasion attending a private Mass with the Pope. He met Mother Teresa of Calcutta. At the Vatican he also met Dr. Robert Fastiggi, who became a lifelong friend and who later, together with Dr. Philip Blosser of Sacred Heart Major Seminary, petitioned the seminary to award Likoudis an honorary doctorate in recognition of his lifetime of service. The degree was conferred in 2020.

"James Likoudis was one of several mentors from whom I was privileged to learn when I was struggling to become more effective in defending and advancing the Catholic faith in my 20s."

— Jeffrey Mirus, PhD, Founder, CatholicCulture.org

Public Life: Television, Town Halls, and Mayoral Politics

Likoudis was a public intellectual in a form that has largely disappeared: he went where the argument was. He debated in town halls that drew standing crowds. He appeared on Geraldo and The Phil Donahue Show — programs that were, in their era, among the sharpest arenas for contested public argument. He appeared regularly on EWTN. His November 6, 1996 appearance on Mother Angelica Live reached hundreds of thousands. His episode on The Journey Home with Marcus Grodi continues to move viewers toward Rome. He ran as a mayoral candidate in Watkins Glen, New York — evidence of a man who understood that faith has a civic dimension and was willing to act on it.

Faith at Home: The Life Behind the Books

Those who knew him personally remember what does not appear in bibliographies. His grandson Andrew Likoudis, who lived with him during his junior and senior years of high school, recalls a man who prayed the Rosary each day without fail, who drove him to school in the mornings and talked theology at the dinner table, who took him to parishes outside the county because he valued beauty in worship too much to settle for what was nearby, who stopped for donuts at Walgreens and went to breakfast with his friends afterward. He and Ruth never fought. They quibbled, affectionately, about whether prayer hands should be folded or held straight.

Recognition and Death

In 2002, Likoudis received the Blessed Frederic Ozanam Award for Catholic Social Action from the Society of Catholic Social Scientists — the organization's recognition for a lay Catholic whose work integrated faith and scholarship in service of the common good. In 2020, Sacred Heart Major Seminary awarded him an honorary Doctorate of Divinity. His works are held at Oxford, Harvard, Princeton, Georgetown, Johns Hopkins, Columbia, Notre Dame, the British Library, and the Library of Congress.

After Ruth's death, James moved to Ann Arbor, Michigan. He died there on September 3, 2024, at the age of 95. His funeral — a Solemn High Mass — was celebrated on September 14 at Assumption Grotto Catholic Church in Detroit. The Society of Catholic Social Scientists described him as "the consummate Catholic intellectual who dedicated his life to finding the light and sharing it in an age in sore need of such illumination."

Family

Ruth, Six Children, and Eighty-Two Descendants

Marriage

James married Ruth (née Hickleton), with whom he shared 71 years of married life. Ruth predeceased him. In his final years, James lived in Ann Arbor, Michigan, where he died September 3, 2024. He is survived by his brother Michael Likoudis.

Descendants

James Likoudis is survived by 35 grandchildren and 47 great-grandchildren — 82 descendants, a living continuation of the faith he embodied. His grandson Andrew Likoudis, who lived with him through high school, founded the Likoudis Legacy Foundation in 2023 to carry his intellectual work forward.
